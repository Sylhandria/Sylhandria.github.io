Functional Networks, Individualized Factors & Cognitive Prediction in Aging

Utilizing resting-state fMRI, I am currently analyzing how reliable and cognitively relevant functional atlas variants are in older adults. With the amount of variants of network atlases that are available to researchers, it is key- in my opinion, to understand if network findings are heavily biased by the toolboxes researchers are using in aging research.

My work focuses on resting-state fMRI data through the use of our multi-site BETTER aging Dataset. Before graduate school, I worked in both cognitive neuroscience and social psychology laboratories in undergrad that heavily influences my desire to ensure we take a more critical look as to how individual differences may influence findings of current neuroimaging methods.

Current Projects
  • 01

    Functional atlas choice & cognitive relevance in aging

    Comparing an age-specific atlas (Atlas 55+) with a standard young-adult atlas to determine which better captures meaningful relationships between functional connectivity and cognition. This project tests how atlas selection shapes the interpretation of brain–behavior links in older adults.

  • 02

    Individualized differences & cross-sectional connectivity–cognition relationships

    Examining how individual differences influence the reliability and cognitive relevance of functional brain atlases in older adults. Cross-sectional analyses assess whether age-appropriate and young-adult atlases differ in their ability to predict cognitive performance from resting-state connectivity, providing insight into how individualized factors shape observed brain–behavior relationships at a single time point.

  • 03

    Individualized differences & longitudinal connectivity–cognition relationships

    Extending the investigation to a longitudinal framework, this aim evaluates whether individualized differences affect the sensitivity of functional atlases in predicting cognitive change over time. Comparisons between age-specific and standard atlases reveal how resting-state connectivity trajectories relate to cognitive trajectories in older adults, clarifying the temporal stability of brain–behavior associations.
